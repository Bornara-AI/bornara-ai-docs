## Overview

This document explains how to use your personal BMO Line of Credit (LOC) to fund business
operations through your dedicated BMO business bank account — including the mechanics of
each draw, interest deductibility, minimum payments, and CRA documentation requirements.

> **Disclaimer:** Based on publicly available CRA guidance. Consult a qualified tax
> professional for advice specific to your situation.

---

## Account Setup

| Account                | Bank | Purpose                                         |
|------------------------|------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Personal LOC           | BMO  | Funding source (do NOT spend from this directly)|
| Business Bank Account  | BMO  | All business spending goes through here          |

**Golden Rule:** Money flows in one direction only:

```text
Personal LOC  →  Business Bank Account  →  Business Expenses
              ↑                          ↑
         (draw)                    (spend)
```

Never pay business expenses directly from the LOC. Always transfer to the business
account first. This creates a clean paper trail that CRA can follow.

## Step-by-Step: How to Make a Draw

### Step 1 — Determine the Need

Before each draw, identify the specific business expenses it will cover:

```text
Example:
  Date:     2026-04-03
  Amount:   $2,500
  Purpose:  Shopify subscription ($39), laptop purchase ($1,800),
            Shopify apps ($50), ad budget ($300), wife wages ($311)
```

### Step 2 — Transfer from LOC to Business Account

1. Log into BMO online banking
2. Transfer the exact amount from Personal LOC → Business Bank Account
3. Screenshot the transfer confirmation (date, amount, from/to accounts)
4. Save the screenshot in your CRA documentation folder

### Step 4 — Spend from Business Account Only

All business purchases, subscriptions, wages, and ad payments come from the business
bank account. This creates:

- Bank statements showing exactly what was purchased
- Clear separation between personal and business spending
- Audit-ready trail for CRA

### Step 5 — Track Interest

BMO charges interest monthly on your LOC balance. The portion of interest attributable
to business draws is 100% deductible.

## Interest Mechanics

### How LOC Interest Works

| Item                    | Detail                                            |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Interest Rate           | ~9–11% variable (BMO, as of March 2026)           |
| Interest Calculation    | Daily on outstanding balance, charged monthly     |
| Minimum Payment         | Interest only (BMO LOC standard)                  |
| Payment Due Date        | Monthly (check your BMO statement)                |

### Interest Cost Examples

| Average Balance | Annual Interest (at 10%) | Monthly Interest |
|-----------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| $5,000          | $500                     | ~$42             |
| $10,000         | $1,000                   | ~$83             |
| $15,000         | $1,500                   | ~$125            |
| $20,000         | $2,000                   | ~$167            |
| $25,000         | $2,500                   | ~$208            |

### Who Pays the Interest

The **business** pays the interest. In practice:

1. BMO charges interest to your personal LOC monthly
2. You transfer the interest amount from the business bank account to the LOC to cover it
3. This transfer is logged as a business expense (T2125 Line 8710 — Interest)
4. The interest payment is **100% tax deductible** because the borrowed money was used
   exclusively for business

### Who Pays the Minimum Payment

- The **minimum payment** on a BMO LOC is typically **interest only**
- Once the business has revenue, use business revenue to pay down principal
- Until then, the interest-only minimum is covered by the business account
  (funded by the LOC itself if needed — this is normal for startups)

## Monthly Routine

Every month, do the following:

### End of Month Checklist

- [ ] Download BMO LOC statement (shows draws, interest, balance)
- [ ] Download BMO business account statement (shows all spending)
- [ ] Record interest charged this month in the expense log
- [ ] Transfer interest amount from business account → LOC (to cover minimum)
- [ ] Update the Draw Tracking Log above
- [ ] Verify cumulative balance is under $25,000
- [ ] File statements in CRA documentation folder

## CRA Deductibility Rules

### What Is Deductible

| Item                     | Deductible? | T2125 Line | Notes                    |
|--------------------------|-------------|------------|--------------------------|
| Interest on LOC (business draws) | Yes | Line 8710 | Must trace to business use |
| Bank fees (business account)     | Yes | Line 8810 | Office expenses            |
| Principal repayment              | No  | —          | Not an expense             |

### What CRA Requires

1. **Trace each draw to a business expense** — the log above does this
2. **LOC must be used exclusively for business** — do NOT use the LOC for
   personal expenses once you start drawing for business
3. **Keep bank statements** — both LOC and business account, every month
4. **Interest statements** — BMO provides these monthly and annually

### Mixed-Use Warning

If you use the same LOC for both personal and business:

- You must prorate the interest (business % only is deductible)
- This is complex and error-prone
- **Strong recommendation:** Use this LOC for business draws only. If you need
  personal credit, use a different credit product.

## Repayment Strategy

### Phase 1: Pre-Revenue (Q1–Q2 2026)

- Business has no revenue yet
- Interest is covered by the LOC itself (draw slightly more to cover interest)
- This is normal for startups — CRA understands businesses borrow to start

### Phase 2: Early Revenue (Q3–Q4 2026)

- Business revenue starts flowing into the business bank account
- Use revenue to cover minimum interest payments
- Principal stays outstanding — focus on growing the business

### Phase 3: Revenue Growth (2027+)

- Business revenue covers interest AND starts paying down principal
- Aim to reduce LOC balance by $3,000–$5,000/year from revenue
- If business becomes highly profitable, pay off LOC aggressively

### If Business Revenue Doesn't Cover Interest

- Your day job income ($94K) easily covers the interest (~$42–$208/month)
- Transfer from personal checking → LOC to cover the difference
- This is NOT a business expense — only interest on business-use draws is deductible

## Risk Scenarios

| Scenario                        | Impact                | Action                       |
|---------------------------------|-----------------------|------------------------------|
| Business fails, $25K outstanding | ~$2,500/yr interest  | Day job covers it; pay down over 2–3 years |
| Interest rate rises to 12%      | +$250–$500/yr cost   | Stay under cap; pay down faster |
| Mixed personal/business use     | Lose deductibility   | Keep LOC business-only        |
| CRA audit questions the draws   | Must show paper trail | Draw log + statements = proof |
